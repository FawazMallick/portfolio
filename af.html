Assistant Housefellow

Carnegie Mellon University · Maggie Mo First-Year Residential Community · 2024–Present

I took this role because I wanted to lead something that wasn't a project — something with people. While carrying a full graduate course load and RA research, I became the operational backbone of a 101-person first-year residential community. This is what I learned about managing people, not managing code.

The Assistant Housefellow (AF) role sits between the Housefellow (a full-time professional staff member) and the Community Advisor (CA) team — the student RAs who run programs and do direct resident engagement. I support both layers: upward to housing leadership, downward to a team of 8 student staff, and horizontally to residents who need intervention or support.

The Maggie Mo First-Year Houses are Carnegie Mellon's primary first-year residential experience — the community where students arrive, adjust to university life, and begin to find their people. Getting that right matters. By mid-semester, our community ranked in the top 3 for residential satisfaction across all 14 first-year communities on campus.

What the Role Actually Involves

Staff Leadership & Development

Weekly 1:1s with student CAs. Set clear expectations, give timely feedback, build accountability for programming goals. Aligned the team on priorities and guided budget decisions collaboratively.

Proactive Wellbeing & Programming

Identified burnout patterns early in the semester. Launched mental health programming and resource awareness initiatives — staff shared personal struggles to normalize vulnerability, built partnerships with campus counseling to connect students to services.

Conflict & Crisis Response

First responder to interpersonal conflicts, mental health concerns, and community disruptions. De-escalated incidents with active listening. Connected students to appropriate campus resources — counseling, Dean of Students, academic support.

Cross-Functional Coordination

Partnered with Residential Education, Academic Affairs, and Student Services on complex cases. Ensured coordination across departments when situations required broader university response.

Operational & Administrative

Managed house budget allocation, staff scheduling, and administrative workflows. Owned the details so the student team could focus entirely on resident engagement and community building.

Confidential Case Management

Handled sensitive student information with appropriate discretion — maintained professional boundaries while ensuring students received follow-up and continuity of care.

The Initiative: Mental Health Programming & Resource Awareness

Two months into the role, I noticed that despite being a vibrant community, first-year students were quietly burning out. Being new, they were hesitant to seek help and often unaware that campus resources existed. Rather than wait for problems to escalate, I called a staff alignment meeting.

What we implemented:

  • Visible mental health resources throughout the residence halls — posters, QR codes, direct contacts for counseling and crisis support
  • Staff-led programming where CAs shared their own struggles and coping strategies, normalizing vulnerability and de-stigmatizing help-seeking
  • Collaborative events with campus counseling, academic coaching, and other student services — bringing resources directly to students where they lived

Outcome: 85% residential satisfaction by mid-semester — top 3 out of 14 residential communities across campus. More importantly, students reported feeling connected to campus resources and to each other.

What's Actually Hard About This

You're a Peer With Supervision Responsibility

The student staff are peers — graduate students, some the same year as me. Having supervision and management responsibility over people who are your intellectual equals, while still being accountable to the professional staff above you, is a particular kind of leadership challenge. You can't just assert hierarchy; you have to build trust and then use it carefully.

Complexity Requires Careful Judgment

Each situation is different. A roommate conflict that surfaces during duty hours requires de-escalation, active listening, and sometimes connecting people to counseling or dean resources. A student mental health concern demands discretion and follow-up. This role taught me what it means to be actually responsible for people — not just responsible for a deliverable that can be handed off or delayed.

Everything Is Contextual

No two situations are the same. A conflict that looks identical on the surface can require completely different responses depending on the history, the personalities, and what each person actually needs. I got better at listening before categorizing — at slowing down the "what do I do?" reflex long enough to actually understand the situation.

The engineering parallel: My research requires understanding a system before designing a controller. The same thing applies here — understand the person before designing the intervention. Jumping to solutions before diagnosing the problem produces bad output in both domains.

How I Approach the Role

Principle 1

Clear expectations up front — set them early, document them, revisit them. Confusion about expectations is the root cause of most avoidable friction.

Principle 2

Operational reliability — handle the details and deadlines so the team doesn't have to. If I drop the administrative ball, my student staff pay the price.

Principle 3

Adaptive approach — things rarely go as planned. Flexibility is not a weakness; it's the ability to get a good outcome on a path that wasn't the original one.

Principle 4

People-first under pressure — keep it professional, calm, and respectful especially when the situation is stressful. Emotional stability in a leader is a resource for the team.

Why This Matters for Engineering

Good engineering happens in teams. The technical skills — control theory, ML, kinematics — are table stakes. What actually determines whether a project ships, whether research gets published, whether a team stays aligned through difficulty, is the human infrastructure around the technical work.

This role gave me practice with that infrastructure: building trust, having hard conversations, managing competing priorities, and keeping people motivated when things are difficult. Those aren't soft skills — they're the skills that make technical skills useful.
