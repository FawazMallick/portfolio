Optimal State Estimator

Probabilistic Foot Placement Prediction from Sequential Bayesian Inference

Abstract

Predicting where a foot will land before it lands is a core challenge in wearable robotics and prosthetic limb control. This project develops a sequential Bayesian state estimator for foot placement prediction during human walking, using IMU-derived kinematic features and a Gaussian prior over gait history. Evaluated on 11,761 steps from 8 able-bodied subjects at 8 treadmill speeds, the framework achieves RMSE ~9.7 cm (Euclidean distance) for subject AB13 — with anterior-posterior prediction substantially more challenging than medial-lateral due to speed-dependent stride length variability.

1. Why Predict Foot Placement?

Current prosthetic and exoskeleton controllers react to mechanical events — they sense ground contact, then respond. This leaves no time for proactive impedance adjustment when a step goes wrong. A predictive controller that knows where the foot will land before touchdown can pre-tune stiffness, damping, or assistive torque to prevent falls and improve stability.

The estimation problem: given the ongoing swing phase hip angle, knee angle, angular velocities captured by an IMU worn at the thigh compute the probability distribution over where the foot will contact the ground, and output the most likely landing position.

2. Approach

Framework summary:

  • Feature extraction from IMU signals (hip/knee angles, angular velocities) during terminal swing phase
  • Ridge regression likelihood models: features → probability over a 101×71 spatial grid
  • Gaussian prior: centered at mean of previous 2 foot placements (captures stride periodicity)
  • Sequential Bayesian posterior update at each step

The posterior over foot landing position x at step i is:

P(xᵢ | zᵢ) ∝ P(zᵢ | xᵢ) · P(xᵢ)

The predicted foot placement is the posterior mode. The prior is updated from each new observation, allowing the estimator to track speed-dependent changes in stride length as walking context accumulates.

Key observation: The Bayesian estimator is most accurate at low walking speeds (RMSE_x as low as 6.2 cm) where gait is most periodic and the prior contributes strongly. At higher speeds, stride length variability increases and AP error grows to ~14 cm. Medial-lateral error remains consistently low (~3 cm) across all speeds, suggesting the ML dimension is inherently more predictable than AP in treadmill walking.
