Context

As part of my Research Assistant work in intent prediction for prosthetic limb users, I performed an offline evaluation of a Bayesian foot placement prediction approach. The method is based on the probability distribution model described in the paper by Peng et al. (IEEE Transactions on Neural Systems and Rehabilitation Engineering, 2021), which predicts where a foot will land during the early swing phase using a wearable IMU sensor.

The approach is fundamentally a state estimator: it maintains a probability distribution over possible foot placement locations on a 2D grid, updating that distribution iteratively as sequential gait features are observed during swing. The weighted center of the distribution is taken as the predicted foot placement coordinate. This is more of a probabilistic/heuristic framework than a learned model in the machine learning sense — the probability map is built from prior stepping patterns, not trained end-to-end.

Based on: Y. Peng et al., "A Probability Distribution Model-Based Approach for Foot Placement Prediction in the Early Swing Phase With a Wearable IMU Sensor," IEEE Transactions on Neural Systems and Rehabilitation Engineering, 2021.

Dataset: Open-source lower limb biomechanics dataset (treadmill walking conditions across multiple speeds and directions).

How It Works

The predictor maintains a grid map of possible foot placements (101 × 71 = 7,171 cells) in the anterior-posterior (AP) and medial-lateral (ML) plane. At the start of each swing phase, a prior distribution is computed from the subject's previous stepping history using compute_prior(). As the swing progresses, sequential IMU-derived features update the probability map, and the predicted landing location is taken as the weighted centroid of the resulting distribution.

In this offline evaluation, pre-trained model objects were loaded from pickle files and applied to treadmill walking data from eight able-bodied subjects (AB06–AB13), covering a total of 11,761 steps across multiple trial conditions (direction, speed, and stance leg). Per-trial and aggregate RMSE metrics were computed for AP displacement, ML displacement, and Euclidean distance to ground truth foot placement.
