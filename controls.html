Course Overview

This page covers three graduate courses forming the core of my controls and robotics foundation at CMU. Robot Dynamics & Analysis (RDA) developed rigorous mathematical tools — screw theory, SE(3), product of exponentials, Jacobians, and constrained dynamics — applied to multi-DOF manipulators. Modern Control Theory (MCT) moved from theory to simulation, implementing PID, LQR, A* planning, and EKF SLAM in Webots across five progressive projects. Optimal Control & Reinforcement Learning (OCRL) closed the loop with trajectory optimization (iLQR) and neural network policies applied to a Two-Wheeled Inverted Pendulum — documented in depth on the TWIP project page.

1. Robot Dynamics & Analysis (RDA)

RDA builds the mathematical machinery for describing and analyzing rigid body motion, from a single joint to a 6-DOF manipulator. The course works in SE(3) — the Lie group of rigid body transformations — rather than Euler angles, which avoids singularities and provides a unified framework for position and orientation.

1.1 Screw Theory Toolkit

The foundational tools translate between different representations of rigid body velocity and pose:

Key functions implemented:

  • twist2rbvel.m — converts a 6-vector twist ξ = [v; ω] to its 4×4 matrix representation ξ̂ in se(3) (the Lie algebra), enabling matrix exponentials via expm(ξ̂·θ)
  • rbvel2twist.m — inverse map: extracts the twist vector from a 4×4 matrix; used to compute Jacobian columns from differentiated FK
  • tform2adjoint.m — computes the 6×6 Adjoint Adg of a homogeneous transformation g ∈ SE(3), enabling wrench and velocity transformations between frames
  • angvel2skew.m / skew2angvel.m — convert between angular velocity 3-vectors and their 3×3 skew-symmetric matrix representations (elements of so(3))

1.2 6-DOF Manipulator — HW4 Part 1

A 6-DOF industrial manipulator (PUMA-style, 3 shoulder joints + 3 wrist joints) was analyzed using both the Denavit-Hartenberg (DH) convention and the product of exponentials (PoE) formulation, and results verified to match symbolically.

At the home configuration (all joint angles zero), the end-effector sits at p = (1407, 0, 1855) mm. The spatial Jacobian Js (6×6) was computed two ways — via direct differentiation of the FK and via the adjoint method — and verified to agree symbolically.

Product of Exponentials formulation: The forward kinematics is expressed as a chain of matrix exponentials of joint twists:

gst(q) = eξ̂₁q₁ · eξ̂₂q₂ · eξ̂₃q₃ · eξ̂₄q₄ · eξ̂₅q₅ · eξ̂₆q₆ · gst(0)

The Adjoint-based Jacobian computes each column as the adjoint-transported joint twist at the current configuration, avoiding repeated symbolic differentiation:

Js,i = Adg₁₂...i(q) · ξi

1.3 2-Link Planar Arm — HW4 Part 2

A 2-link planar arm (lengths l₁, l₂) was used to derive and compare the spatial and body Jacobians, and to identify manipulator singularities.

  • Spatial Jacobian Js (6×2): columns express joint velocities in the spatial (fixed world) frame
  • Body Jacobian Jb (6×2): related by Jb = Adgst⁻¹ · Js, expressing the same velocities in the end-effector body frame
  • Singularity: the arm loses a degree of freedom when θ₂ = 0° (fully extended) or θ₂ = 180° (fully folded) — the Jacobian loses rank and the manipulator cannot generate velocity radially inward or outward

1.4 Planar Grasping — HW5

Grasping theory was applied to a planar object gripped at two contact points with Coulomb friction (friction coefficient μf).

Grasp map construction:

  • Contact frames rotated from object frame via rotation matrices Roc1, Roc2
  • Each grasp block: Gi = AdTi · Bci, where Bci ∈ ℝ6×2 is the contact wrench basis (normal + tangential friction)
  • Full grasp map: G = [G₁ G₂] ∈ ℝ6×4
  • Reduced grasp map Gp (forces + yaw) has rank 3 → form-closure satisfied for friction ≥ 0

The null space of G (dim = 1) represents internal force modes — contact forces that cancel at the object level but can be used to squeeze the grasp and increase friction headroom without disturbing object equilibrium.

1.5 Constrained Dynamics

Contact mechanics and constrained dynamics were analyzed via block-structured linear systems. When a manipulator makes contact with a rigid environment, the constrained equations of motion take the form:

⎡ M   −Aᵀ ⎤ ⎡ q̈ ⎤   ⎡ τ − C(q,q̇)q̇ ⎤
⎣ A     0   ⎦ ⎣ λ ⎦ = ⎣     0           ⎦

where A is the constraint Jacobian, λ are the Lagrange multiplier contact forces, and M is the mass matrix. This system was solved numerically for acceleration and contact force, and the results compared for different contact mode assumptions (sticking vs. sliding).

2. Modern Control Theory (MCT) — Webots Projects

MCT applied state-space control, planning, and estimation in Webots — a 3D robot simulation environment — across five progressive projects. Each project built on the previous, from basic PID control to full SLAM.

Project 1
Vehicle Path Tracking — Tesla Model 3 Bicycle Model

Implemented longitudinal PID speed control (target: 5.0 m/s) with rolling resistance feedforward (F_roll = 0.019·m·g). Lateral control via pure pursuit — steering toward a look-ahead point 40 waypoints ahead on the reference path.

Vehicle parameters: m=1,888.6 kg, Iz=25,854 kg·m², lf=1.55 m, lr=1.39 m (bicycle model approximation).

Tools: Python · Webots · PID · Pure Pursuit
Project 2
Optimal Trajectory Generation — Lane Change / Parking

Generated optimal vehicle trajectories for constrained maneuvers (lane change and parallel parking) using polynomial trajectory planning with boundary conditions on position, velocity, and acceleration at initial and final states.

Compared greedy vs. optimal controllers — the optimal approach satisfied comfort constraints (bounded jerk) that the reactive controller violated.

Tools: Python · Webots · Polynomial Trajectory · scipy.linalg
Project 3
A* Path Planning + LQR Trajectory Following

Implemented A* on a 2D occupancy grid (0.05 m resolution) to find minimum-cost collision-free waypoints. An LQR controller then tracked the planned path in closed-loop, rejecting disturbances and maintaining stability along the full trajectory.

Demonstrated clean separation of planning (global, offline) from control (local, online).

Tools: Python · Webots · A* (heapq) · LQR · scipy.linalg
Project 4
EKF SLAM — Simultaneous Localization and Mapping

Implemented full Extended Kalman Filter SLAM. State vector augmented to include robot pose [X, Y, ψ] and 2D landmark positions [xL,1, yL,1, …, xL,n, yL,n], totaling (3+2n) states.

Nonlinear motion model f(x,u) and measurement model h(x) linearized via Jacobians at each timestep. Landmark association performed by nearest-neighbor gating.

Tools: Python · Webots · EKF · Jacobian linearization · numpy

EKF SLAM — State & Measurement Model Detail

State vector: μ = [X, Y, ψ, xL,1, yL,1, …, xL,n, yL,n]T ∈ ℝ3+2n

Motion model:

⎡ Xt+1 ⎤   ⎡ Xt + Δt(ẋ cosψ − ẏ sinψ) ⎤
⎢ Yt+1 ⎥ = ⎢ Yt + Δt(ẋ sinψ + ẏ cosψ) ⎥
⎣ ψt+1 ⎦   ⎣         ψt + Δt·ψ̇             ⎦

Measurement model: range + bearing from robot to each observed landmark; Jacobian Ht linearizes h(x) at current estimate for the Kalman update step.

Covariance propagation: Σt|t−1 = F·Σt−1·FT + W, where W is the process noise covariance and F = ∂f/∂x evaluated at μt−1.

3. Optimal Control & Reinforcement Learning (OCRL)

OCRL advanced from state-space design into nonlinear trajectory optimization and neural network policy learning. The centerpiece project applied four control frameworks progressively to a Two-Wheeled Inverted Pendulum (TWIP) — a canonical underactuated system where classical methods fail for large-angle maneuvers.

Key results from the TWIP project:

  • LQR stabilization: K = [−0.7958, −0.7138, −51.7877, −4.6309], converging from θ₀=0.1 rad within 5 s
  • Kalman Filter: estimated state converges to truth within ~2 s under sensor noise (σ²_θ = 0.5 rad²)
  • iLQR drag-race: target φ* = 2π rad achieved in ~3.0 s; converged in 7 iterations
  • Neural network policy (4→2→1, 13 params): fine-tuned to 42 ms balance time — 7× longer than linear gains

The course also covered surrogate-based optimization (neural network surrogate for the 4D Rosenbrock function), policy gradient methods, and the trade-off between sample efficiency and asymptotic performance in model-free RL.
