1. Finite Element Analysis

1.1 Course Overview

FEA coursework developed the mathematical and computational foundation for structural analysis of medical device components. Rather than relying on commercial solvers as black boxes, the course required building the FEA pipeline from first principles — from mesh generation through global stiffness assembly and stress recovery — giving insight into what software like ANSYS and Abaqus are actually computing.

⚙️

2D Structural FEA

Custom MATLAB solver using BristolFE framework. Plane stress and plane strain formulations; hexagonal and unstructured meshes.

🌡️

1D Heat Transfer FEA

Direct stiffness method from scratch. Three-layer composite (glass–air–glass); conduction + convection terms; Neumann BCs.

🦴

Hip Implant Fatigue

ISO 7206-4 axial fatigue and ISO 7206-6 bending load simulation. Stress concentration identification and fatigue life estimation.

1.2 2D Structural FEA Pipeline

The BristolFE MATLAB library implements the complete finite element pipeline. Working through it step by step — understanding what each function computes and why — built intuition that carries over to interpreting commercial solver outputs critically.

1

Mesh Generation

Define geometry and element size. Generate node positions and element connectivity. Structured (rectangular/hexagonal) meshes for uniform geometries; unstructured Mesh2D for curved or complex shapes.

2

Material Properties & Element Stiffness

Specify Young's modulus and Poisson's ratio. Compute element stiffness matrices Ke via plane stress or plane strain constitutive law. Evaluate at Gauss quadrature points.

3

Global Stiffness Assembly

Map element stiffness into global K matrix using element-to-global DOF connectivity table. Enforce symmetry and sparsity for efficiency.

4

Boundary Conditions & Loading

Apply displacement constraints at support nodes (frictionless base = vertical DOF fixed). Apply distributed load or prescribed displacement on loaded surfaces.

5

Solve → Stress Recovery

Solve K·u = f for displacement field u. Back-compute element stresses (σxx, σyy, σxy). Visualize as color-mapped contour plots. Compare σmax to material yield strength.

1.3 Hip Implant Fatigue Simulation — ISO 7206 Standards

A hip implant femoral stem was analyzed under clinically relevant loading conditions, following the ISO 7206 standard test series used in regulatory submissions:

StandardLoad CaseObjective
ISO 7206-4 Axial fatigue — 2.3 kN at 5 Hz, 5×10⁶ cycles Identify stem failure under repetitive vertical load
ISO 7206-6 Bending fatigue — cantilever setup, 10 mm offset Assess neck-stem junction stress concentration under bending

Key finding: The neck-stem junction exhibits the highest von Mises stress concentration under ISO 7206-6 bending load — consistent with clinical literature on implant fatigue fracture sites. The FEA stress map directly informed the region targeted for material upgrades and surface finish improvement in the regulatory design review.

What FEA taught me: The same mathematical structure — assemble element contributions into a global system, apply boundary conditions, solve — appears across numerical engineering. Understanding it from first principles means I can read commercial FEA output critically rather than just accepting it. For a medical device, that distinction matters: the regulatory reviewer expects you to understand why your simulation is valid, not just that it passed.

2. Medical Device Regulatory Bootcamp

2.1 Overview

The Medical Device Regulatory Bootcamp was a capstone course that simulated the full lifecycle of a Class III medical device startup pursuing FDA 510(k) clearance for a hip implant. Teams operated as if they were a real medical device company — responsible for the entire quality system and regulatory dossier from concept through mock submission.

Device classification: Class III active implantable device (hip femoral stem). Regulatory pathway: FDA 510(k) premarket notification, citing predicate device(s) with substantial equivalence argument. Quality framework: ISO 13485:2016 Medical Devices — Quality Management Systems.

2.3 Regulatory Pathway — FDA 510(k)

The 510(k) pathway requires demonstrating substantial equivalence — that the new device is at least as safe and effective as a legally marketed predicate device. The course walked through the full submission logic:

  • Predicate identification: search the FDA 510(k) database for cleared devices with the same intended use and technological characteristics
  • Substantial equivalence argument: side-by-side comparison of new device vs. predicate on materials, dimensions, performance, and labeling
  • Performance testing: reference to consensus standards (ISO 7206-4, ISO 7206-6) to satisfy bench testing requirements
  • Biocompatibility: material toxicological risk assessment per ISO 10993 series
  • Design controls: design input/output traceability, verification & validation plan per 21 CFR Part 820

2.4 ISO 13485 Quality Audit

An internal quality audit was conducted against ISO 13485:2016, covering the management, resource, product realization, and monitoring/measurement clauses. Audit findings were documented in CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action) forms:

  • Major nonconformity: inadequate traceability between design inputs and verification activities (§7.3.6) — CAPA required within 30 days
  • Minor nonconformity: supplier qualification records not current (§7.4.1) — corrective action and re-audit scheduled
  • Observation: SOPs not reviewed on annual schedule (§4.2.4) — preventive action initiated

2.5 Risk Management — ISO 14971

Risk management followed ISO 14971:2019. The process: hazard identification → risk estimation (severity × probability) → risk control → residual risk evaluation → benefit-risk judgment.

Key takeaway: The regulatory bootcamp made clear that FDA clearance is not a single submission event — it is an evidence-based argument assembled over the entire design lifecycle. Every decision in device design generates a document; that document is what the reviewer reads. Getting comfortable with this paper trail early is what distinguishes engineers who can move a device to market from those who can only design one.

3. Biomechanics & Gait Analysis

3.1 Why Biomechanics Underlies My Robotics Work

You cannot design a wearable device for a human without understanding how that human moves. The exoskeleton project required knowing what happens at the hip joint during a slip perturbation. The prosthetics research required understanding how gait phase maps onto IMU sensor signals. These are not optional extras — they are core design constraints.

3.2 Normal Gait Cycle

The normal walking gait cycle is divided into stance phase (~60% of cycle: heel strike → foot flat → midstance → heel-off → toe-off) and swing phase (~40%: initial swing → midswing → terminal swing). The transition between them — toe-off — is the event that triggers prosthetic swing phase control and defines the feature extraction window for foot placement prediction.

Phase% Gait CycleKey EventRelevance to Device Design
Heel Strike0%Foot contacts groundGround reaction force onset; FSR trigger event
Loading Response0–10%Weight transfer to stance legPeak quadriceps demand; implant loading
Midstance10–30%Single limb supportHip abductor stabilization — exoskeleton target
Terminal Stance30–50%Heel risePlantarflexion power burst; prosthetic push-off
Toe-Off60%Foot leaves groundSwing phase initiation; foot placement prediction window opens
Swing Phase60–100%Limb advancementIMU-based intent prediction; hip/knee angle feature extraction

3.3 Joint Mechanics & Ground Reaction Forces

  • Ground Reaction Forces (GRF): the ground's reaction to body weight and dynamic loading. The characteristic double-bump GRF profile (loading peak ~1.2 BW, push-off peak ~1.1 BW) is used to validate OpenSim simulations against force plate measurements
  • Hip abductor function: during single-leg stance, the hip abductors (gluteus medius/minimus) prevent pelvic drop on the swing side. Perturbation of this mechanism is the target of the exoskeleton actuator
  • Inverse dynamics: from GRF and kinematics, compute joint moments. Used to set torque targets for exoskeleton actuation and validate OpenSim simulation magnitudes
  • EMG patterns: timing of key muscle activation relative to gait events (e.g., tibialis anterior peaks at heel strike; soleus peaks at push-off). Used to interpret IMU signals in context of neuromuscular control

3.4 OpenSim — Musculoskeletal Simulation

OpenSim (Stanford) provides validated musculoskeletal models for forward and inverse dynamics simulation. For the exoskeleton project, a full-body gait model was used to:

  • Simulate exoskeleton contact forces using elastic foundation contact (stiffness 60,000 N/m; damping 3,000 N·s/m)
  • Convert BNO055 IMU quaternion streams to joint angles as simulation inputs
  • Validate simulated F1 module loads against FSR measurements (≈85% accuracy on peak timing)
  • Identify the 100–200 ms response window during early swing in which perturbation correction is both possible and beneficial
OpenSim simulation methodology, elastic foundation contact parameters, and validation results are detailed on the exoskeleton research page. Exoskeleton Research →

Biomechanics as a design constraint: The 100–200 ms perturbation correction window identified in OpenSim directly set the latency requirement for the FSR detection algorithm (observed: <150 ms response time). Similarly, understanding that foot placement during swing is determined by terminal swing kinematics — not earlier — defined the feature extraction window for the prosthetics research. Domain knowledge shapes what the algorithm must do before you write a single line of code.
