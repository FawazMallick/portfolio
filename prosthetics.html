Prosthetic Leg Gait Prediction

Current RA Research • CMU Human Robotics Lab

Over 2 million lower-limb amputees exist globally. Most use prosthetics that are reactive — they respond to the user's current motion. But what if prosthetics could predict the user's next step? What if they anticipated intent? This is the grand problem I'm working on: building machine learning and statistical models that predict foot placement from IMU sensor data, enabling the next generation of intelligent prosthetics.

The Grand Problem

Why Prediction Matters

Prosthetic limb users face a constant challenge: their device can only react to what's happening right now. By the time the prosthetic detects a slip or an uneven surface, the user's body is already destabilizing. This reactive approach leads to falls, injuries, and reduced quality of life.

Prediction changes this. If we can predict where a user intends to place their foot — just 200-500 milliseconds in advance — the prosthetic can pre-adjust its stiffness, damping, and actuation. The user can recover from slips. They can navigate uneven terrain confidently. They can live without fear.

The Core Question: Can we use IMU sensor data and biomechanical models to predict foot placement coordinates with enough accuracy and speed to enable real-time prosthetic control?

The Challenge

  • Sensor noise and drift over time
  • High variability across different users and walking conditions
  • Latency constraints (< 100ms for real-time control)
  • Generalization to novel environments
  • Safety requirements (can't make wrong predictions)

Exploring Different Approaches

There's no single "right" way to solve this problem. Researchers have explored multiple methodologies, each with different tradeoffs:

Inverse Kinematics

Traditional biomechanics: solve the inverse problem to find joint angles from desired foot position. Fast, interpretable, but assumes ideal geometry.

Physics-Based Models

Model gait dynamics with ODEs. Requires accurate model of user's body. Interpretable but computationally expensive to calibrate per-user.

Statistical Learning (Regression)

Ridge regression, polynomial features, gaussian processes. Data-driven, no need to hand-craft models. Can generalize if trained on diverse data.

Deep Learning

LSTMs, CNNs, transformers on time-series IMU data. Can learn complex patterns. Powerful but requires lots of data and is less interpretable.

Bayesian Inference

Probabilistic modeling with uncertainty quantification. Principled, gives confidence estimates, naturally handles noise. Current focus of my research.

Hybrid Methods

Combine physics-based models with learned corrections. Balance interpretability and data-driven flexibility.

Current Work: Bayesian Foot Placement Prediction

The Dataset

I work with motion capture and IMU data collected from subjects walking on a treadmill under various conditions:

  • Subjects: Multiple participants across different walking speeds and patterns
  • Sensors: Inertial Measurement Units (IMU) on both legs, plus motion capture markers
  • Ground truth: Precise foot placement coordinates from motion capture system
  • Conditions: Normal walking, variable speeds, controlled perturbations

The Method: Bayesian Inference

Why Bayesian? Because we want to quantify uncertainty. In medical/safety-critical applications, knowing "I'm 70% confident" is different from "I'm 95% confident." Bayesian methods give us that.

The Pipeline:
1. Feature extraction from IMU data (acceleration, angular velocity, orientation)
2. Gait phase identification (when is the user in swing vs stance phase?)
3. Bayesian model that predicts foot placement given current state
4. Uncertainty quantification (confidence in the prediction)

Offline Evaluation Results

Current results on offline validation (Bayes_Test_Offline.ipynb):

  • Prediction accuracy: Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) < 50mm across test subjects
  • Consistency: High accuracy maintained across different walking speeds
  • Feature importance: Identified which IMU features drive predictions
  • Robustness: Validation on held-out test data shows good generalization

Note: These are offline, post-hoc evaluations on recorded data. Real-time deployment is the next frontier.

Challenges & Future Directions

Current Challenges

  • Online Learning: Models trained offline must adapt to new users in real-time
  • Sensor Drift: IMU calibration drifts over hours of use
  • Subject Variability: Each person walks differently; personalization is key
  • Latency: Must predict within 50-100ms for real-time control feasibility
  • Generalization: Training on treadmills, testing in real-world environments (stairs, uneven terrain)

Future: Multi-Modal Sensing

Current work uses IMU alone. The next phase combines multiple sensors:

  • EMG (Electromyography): Muscle activation signals from the residual limb or intact leg
  • Force Sensors: Ground reaction forces and socket pressure
  • Vision: User's gaze direction or environmental context (if camera available)
  • Proprioceptive Feedback: Combining sensory modalities for richer understanding

Multi-modal sensing is more robust and could dramatically improve prediction accuracy and adaptability.

Vision

The ultimate goal is a prosthetic system that:

  • Predicts user intent 200-500ms in advance
  • Adapts to new users and environments online
  • Provides confident, safe predictions with uncertainty quantification
  • Works across diverse terrains and walking patterns
  • Integrates with actuation for real-time, intelligent control

This is multi-year research. I'm excited to push it forward.

Why This Matters

Beyond the technical challenge, this work has real human impact. Every prediction, every model improvement directly affects someone's quality of life. It's why I chose to focus on human-robot interaction over pure robotics theory.

When you're building a system that people wear on their bodies, your engineering decisions matter in ways they don't in a lab setting. Safety, reliability, and the user's experience aren't optional — they're fundamental.

This research sits at the intersection of robotics, machine learning, biomechanics, and human factors. It requires understanding the physics, the algorithms, and the humans using these systems. That's the kind of problem I love.
