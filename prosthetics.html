Intent Prediction for Prosthetic Leg Application on Amputee

Sequential Bayesian Foot Placement Prediction from IMU-Derived Gait Features

Abstract

Lower-limb amputees face elevated fall risk due to the reactive nature of current prosthetic controllers. This work investigates a Bayesian approach to predicting foot placement location one step ahead from IMU-derived kinematic features. Data were collected from 8 able-bodied subjects (11,761 total steps) during treadmill walking at 8 speeds. A sequential Bayesian update over a spatial grid achieves a mean foot placement prediction error of RMSE 9.11 cm anterior-posterior and 3.35 cm medial-lateral for subject AB13. Anterior-posterior error dominates across all subjects, and a consistent asymmetry between left and right leg predictions is observed. This work is part of a broader research program targeting real-time intent prediction for prosthetic limb control.

1. Introduction

Clinical motivation: Approximately 2 million people in the United States are living with lower-limb amputation [1]. Falls are a leading secondary consequence — lower-limb amputees fall at nearly twice the rate of age-matched controls, often during transitional locomotion tasks [2]. Current prosthetic controllers are fundamentally reactive: they adjust after ground contact is detected, leaving no margin for pre-emptive stability corrections.

A predictive prosthetic controller — one that anticipates foot landing position before the swing phase completes — could enable preemptive impedance adjustments and reduce falls. The central challenge is extracting reliable foot placement intent from the sensor suite available on or near a prosthetic device, in real time.

This work develops and evaluates a Bayesian inference framework for foot placement prediction. Motion capture ground-truth and IMU-derived features from able-bodied subjects serve as a surrogate for the amputee population, establishing a performance baseline before clinical translation. Intent recognition from such features has been studied in the context of locomotion mode identification [3] and powered prosthetic control [4]; this work focuses specifically on the spatial prediction of foot landing position.

This research is conducted in collaboration with the Neuromechanical Systems Lab (PI: Dr. Hartmut Geyer), Carnegie Mellon University Robotics Institute.

2. Experimental Protocol

Dataset:

  • 8 able-bodied subjects: AB06–AB13
  • Treadmill walking at 8 speeds (trials 01_01 through 08_01)
  • Both legs recorded (left and right)
  • Total steps: 11,761 across all subjects and trials
  • Ground-truth foot placement from motion capture (AP and ML coordinates)
  • IMU signals: hip/knee angles and angular velocities, sampled during swing phase

3. Methodology

The prediction framework operates in two stages: feature extraction from IMU signals during terminal swing, followed by a sequential Bayesian update over a spatial foot placement grid.

Pipeline (high level):

  • Feature extraction: IMU-derived kinematic signals (hip/knee angles, angular velocities) processed during the swing phase
  • Likelihood models: Pre-trained Ridge regression models map features to probability distributions over the spatial grid
  • Bayesian prior: A Gaussian prior centered at the mean of the previous two foot placements — captures gait periodicity
  • Posterior update: Bayes' theorem combines prior and likelihood; the posterior mode is the predicted foot placement
  • Grid: 101 × 71 cells over anterior-posterior (AP) and medial-lateral (ML) space

Sequential Bayesian Update

At each step i, the prior P(xᵢ) is centered at the mean of the two most recent placements. The posterior is:

P(xᵢ | zᵢ) ∝ P(zᵢ | xᵢ) · P(xᵢ)

For the first two steps the prior is uninformative. From step 3 onward, the prior tracks speed-dependent stride adaptations as gait context accumulates.

Key findings:

  • AP error (9.1 cm) dominates ML error (3.3 cm) — stride length varies more with speed than stride width, making the AP direction harder to predict
  • Right leg shows systematically higher AP error than left leg across most speeds — consistent with limb dominance effects and possible sensor asymmetry
  • Low-speed left-leg trials show lowest RMSE (6.2–7.7 cm) — the Bayesian prior works best when stride length is consistent and gait is periodic
  • Error increases with speed (left leg trial 07_01: 13.85 cm) — reflecting increased stride variability at faster walking speeds

5. Future Directions

The current framework establishes a Bayesian baseline for foot placement prediction in healthy subjects. Several directions are being pursued toward clinical deployment:

  • Multi-modal sensing: Adding EMG (muscle activity) and plantar pressure alongside IMU to capture neuromuscular intent earlier in the gait cycle
  • Subject-adaptive online updating: Real-time parameter adaptation to address inter-subject variability without recalibration sessions
  • Transfer to prosthetic users: Validation on trans-tibial and trans-femoral amputees, where gait pattern and sensor placement differ from able-bodied subjects
  • Deep learning comparison: Evaluating LSTM and temporal CNN architectures against the Bayesian baseline for sequence-to-sequence foot placement regression
  • Real-time embedded deployment: Latency and feasibility evaluation on microcontroller platforms typical of powered prosthetic knees and ankles
