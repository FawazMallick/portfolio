Title: Estimating Human-Exoskeleton Interaction Forces Using an Instrumented Thigh Brace and OpenSim

Venue: IFAC PapersOnLine (2025)

Keywords: Exoskeletons, FSR sensors, human–machine interaction forces, OpenSim simulation

PDF: Download Paper

What this work is about

This work builds directly on the earlier Exoskeleton Project and the OpenSim Gait Simulation.

Physical human – exoskeleton interaction forces are critical for safety, comfort, and effective assistive control. However, these forces are rarely measured directly in most exoskeleton systems.

This work introduces a wearable sensing system using an instrumented thigh brace to measure interface forces and combines those measurements with a contact simulation model built in OpenSim to estimate interaction forces between the human and exoskeleton during walking.

How we did it

  • Instrumented thigh brace: A compliant fabric brace was developed with embedded force-sensitive resistors (FSRs) distributed around the thigh to measure contact forces.
  • Custom sensor housings: 3D-printed casings were designed to prevent sensor bending and maintain consistent contact with the curved thigh surface.
  • Calibration and validation: The sensors were calibrated using a load frame and validated using stationary tests where subjects exerted forces in multiple directions.
  • Dynamic walking experiments: Three subjects performed walking trials to measure interaction forces during natural gait.
  • OpenSim simulation: A preliminary human–exoskeleton contact model was created using OpenSim to estimate interaction forces using simulated contact elements and IMU-driven hip motion.

Key outcomes

  • The instrumented brace captured measurable interface forces during walking and revealed correlations between hip motion and interaction forces.
  • Results showed inter-subject variability driven by differences in thigh size, brace fit, and gait dynamics.
  • OpenSim simulations produced force trends that aligned with the experimental data at selected contact points.
  • The approach provides a practical tool for studying human–exoskeleton interaction forces and improving exoskeleton design and control strategies.

Why it matters

A major challenge in wearable robotics is not just generating assistive torque but doing so safely and comfortably. Measuring and modeling interface forces enables better ergonomic design, more adaptive control strategies, and improved safety for assistive devices.
